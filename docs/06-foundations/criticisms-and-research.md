# Strong Objections and Research Program

PBMM should invite serious attack.

A theory becomes stronger when its creator states where it may fail, what evidence would challenge it, and which claims remain metaphorical or provisional.

## Core objections

### Why these three domains?

PBMM proposes Value, Stability, and Resources as a useful compression.

But why not:

- four domains;
- five domains;
- a hierarchy;
- a circular value structure;
- or a network without privileged axes?

The three-domain structure should be retained only if it explains more with less distortion than competing decompositions.

### Are the six priorities truly distinct?

Dignity may overlap with Security.

Recognition may overlap with Harmony.

Acquisition may function as a route to Dignity.

Comfort may function as a form of Security.

PBMM claims that these motives can still be distinguished by their nearest direct aim, but that distinction requires testing.

### Are the poles really polar?

Dignity and Recognition can both be high.

Security and Harmony can reinforce one another.

Acquisition and Comfort can form a healthy cycle.

The model therefore uses polarity to describe directional contrast rather than strict mutual exclusion.

Research must determine whether the paired structure adds explanatory value beyond merely listing six motives.

### Is masculine/feminine language necessary?

The symbolic polarity carries biological, mythic, and cultural information, but it may also introduce confusion or ideological resistance.

Research questions include:

- Does the language improve memory and prediction?
- Does it distort observer judgment?
- Do alternative labels preserve the same compression?
- Does the symbolism travel across cultures?
- Are sex differences relevant, incidental, or misleading at the level of this model?

The language should remain provisional.

### Are archetypes discovered or imposed?

An archetype may reveal a recurring human structure, or it may simply be a memorable story attached to an arbitrary pairing.

A useful test is whether independent observers:

- identify similar configurations;
- distinguish neighboring archetypes;
- predict similar tradeoffs;
- and recognize the configuration before being told the name.

### Does vector language add real value?

Terms such as vector, geometry, magnitude, attractor, and projection can clarify structure.

They can also lend mathematical prestige without empirical substance.

The vector interpretation earns its place only if it helps distinguish:

- direction from intensity;
- state from attractor;
- local from global capture;
- movement from fixed type;
- and competing hypotheses from categorical labels.

### Is PBMM predictive?

A model that explains everything after the fact explains very little.

PBMM should eventually generate prospective predictions such as:

- which priority a person will sacrifice under a specified conflict;
- what type of argument will alter their behavior;
- which stressor will activate a secondary mask;
- which neglected function would restore flexibility;
- or how an institution will respond to a particular threat.

### How does it avoid confirmation bias?

Archetypes are broad and memorable. This makes them powerful and dangerous.

An observer can easily reinterpret every behavior as confirmation.

The method must therefore require:

- alternative hypotheses;
- disconfirming evidence;
- explicit confidence levels;
- separation of state and attractor;
- and observer agreement studies.

### Does it confuse morality with motivation?

The same priority can produce virtue or corruption.

Security can protect or oppress.

Harmony can reconcile or suppress.

Dignity can preserve integrity or become pride.

Recognition can affirm value or become vanity.

PBMM should describe the priority structure before making moral judgment about its expression.

### Can it scale from individuals to civilizations?

Groups are not single minds.

Aggregate applications risk personifying institutions, political parties, and civilizations.

A stronger social model would distinguish:

- distribution of individual priorities;
- institutional incentives;
- role specialization;
- elite narratives;
- public rhetoric;
- and actual resource flows.

Large-scale archetypal descriptions should be treated as aggressive compressions, not literal group psyches.

## Falsification and revision conditions

PBMM should be revised or abandoned if evidence shows that:

- independent observers cannot distinguish the six priorities above chance;
- the proposed pairings do not improve prediction;
- governance order cannot be reliably inferred;
- archetypal labels reduce rather than improve observer agreement;
- a simpler established model predicts the same outcomes better;
- the three-domain compression systematically excludes major motives;
- or the framework produces confident interpretations without useful disconfirmation.

## Research program

### Phase 1: Semantic clarification

Objectives:

- define each priority precisely;
- identify overlap and boundary cases;
- collect examples and counterexamples;
- test whether different cultures interpret the terms similarly;
- and refine the naming of archetypal regions.

Outputs:

- canonical glossary;
- decision rules;
- ambiguity catalog;
- and translated or culturally adapted vocabularies.

### Phase 2: Observer agreement

Give independent observers the same behavioral material and ask them to estimate:

- dominant domain;
- governing priority;
- supporting priority;
- primary archetypal hypothesis;
- and confidence.

Measure:

- agreement;
- confusion between neighboring categories;
- effect of training;
- and whether archetypal names improve or bias classification.

### Phase 3: Self-report instrument

Develop provisional items for all six priorities.

Requirements:

- distinguish intensity from governance;
- avoid obvious moral desirability;
- include forced tradeoffs;
- measure context variation;
- and compare self-report with behavior.

The instrument should not be released as a diagnostic test before validation.

### Phase 4: Behavioral prediction

Test whether inferred configurations predict choices under conflict.

Examples:

- Dignity versus Harmony;
- Security versus Recognition;
- Acquisition versus Comfort;
- short-term Comfort versus long-term Security;
- social Recognition versus private integrity.

Predictions should be specified before observing outcomes.

### Phase 5: Longitudinal dynamics

Track individuals over time to examine:

- stability of priority weights;
- context-sensitive movement;
- recurring attractors;
- transitions produced by stress;
- effects of deliberate mask practice;
- and whether increased repertoire correlates with wellbeing or performance.

### Phase 6: Embodiment and acting

Ask trained actors to embody specified configurations without archetypal names, then test whether observers recognize the intended pattern.

This could examine whether priorities reliably alter:

- posture;
- gaze;
- movement;
- voice;
- tempo;
- interpersonal distance;
- and scene objectives.

Actors may also reveal where two supposedly distinct archetypes are not experientially separable.

### Phase 7: Institutional analysis

Compare organizations with different incentive systems.

Questions:

- Which priorities are rewarded?
- Which are suppressed?
- Which functions become specialized?
- What feedback loops produce capture?
- Which neglected functions predict failure?

This should be grounded in institutional behavior, not only symbolic analogy.

### Phase 8: Comparative models

Compare PBMM against established frameworks involving:

- human values;
- motives and needs;
- attachment;
- agency and communion;
- threat, drive, and soothing;
- personality dimensions;
- and moral foundations.

The objective is not to prove that PBMM contains everything. It is to identify what is genuinely new, redundant, or better expressed elsewhere.

## Documentation standards

Future repository contributions should distinguish:

### Structural claim

A definition belonging to the canonical model.

### Interpretive hypothesis

A plausible implication not yet established.

### Illustrative example

A scene or case designed to make the structure understandable.

### Public-behavior analysis

A probabilistic interpretation of public evidence, not private diagnosis.

### Speculative extension

An application to civilization, theology, AI, politics, or other domains that may inspire research but should not silently modify the core.

## The standard of success

PBMM succeeds only if it does more than generate compelling language.

It should eventually help observers:

- discriminate motives more reliably;
- predict tradeoffs more accurately;
- communicate across motivational differences;
- recognize captured loops;
- and deliberately expand individual or institutional repertoire.

The model should remain corrigible.

> **Its purpose is not to win permanent sovereignty for one conceptual system, but to improve perception and integration.**
