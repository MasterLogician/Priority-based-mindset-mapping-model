# Archetype Naming Principles

PBMM should prefer inherited symbolic forms over freshly invented personality labels.

The purpose of an archetypal name is not merely to identify a structure. It is to activate a compressed body of cultural, mythic, dramatic, and embodied knowledge.

## Primary rule

> **Prefer a timeless archetype that civilization has already spent centuries compressing.**

A strong name should evoke more than a task.

It should carry:

- motive;
- posture;
- social relation;
- virtue;
- shadow;
- ritual or mythic associations;
- and a recognizable way of seeing the world.

## Structural truth comes first

A powerful symbol is not enough. It must accurately map the priority pairing and direction of governance.

The naming sequence should be:

1. Define the structural pairing.
2. Define which priority governs.
3. Describe the core movement without archetypal language.
4. Search inherited symbolic traditions for the closest figure.
5. Test the figure against virtue, shadow, embodiment, and memorable scenes.
6. Preserve the structural identifier even when the symbolic name changes.

## Symbolic sources

Useful naming domains include:

- myth;
- epic;
- religion;
- esoteric traditions;
- tarot;
- court and household roles;
- ritual offices;
- dramatic stock characters;
- traditional crafts;
- and ancient economic or civilizational functions.

No one tradition should be forced to supply the entire table. The objective is not to make PBMM secretly identical to tarot, Jung, alchemy, or any single mythology.

## Tests for a strong name

A candidate should pass most of the following tests.

### Immediate image

Does the word produce a recognizable person, posture, or scene?

### Structural fit

Does the figure actually express the governing and supporting priority in the correct order?

### Virtue and shadow

Does the image contain both a legitimate function and a characteristic corruption?

### Embodiment

Could an actor enter the mask through breath, gaze, rhythm, voice, stance, and intention?

### Cultural depth

Does the symbol connect to inherited stories, rituals, roles, or esoteric knowledge?

### Distinctness

Can it be clearly distinguished from neighboring archetypes?

### Neutrality of range

Can the figure express healthy, immature, corrupt, tragic, and transformed forms without the name itself deciding the moral verdict?

### Memorability

Does it belong naturally in a chant, matrix, story, or symbolic family?

## Weak naming patterns

Avoid names that are:

- merely occupational when a deeper symbolic role exists;
- sanitized to remove all shadow;
- technical descriptions disguised as archetypes;
- modern branding language;
- moral judgments rather than functional forms;
- or chosen only because they create a tidy male/female pair.

For example, “Builder” communicates function but opens a thinner symbolic library than “Architect.” “Guardian” is intelligible but may be less charged than a sacred defender such as “Paladin.”

This does not prove that the stronger-sounding candidate is correct. It identifies where further symbolic research is justified.

## Names should remain provisional

PBMM is still discovering the best symbols for several structural regions.

A candidate name should be marked provisional when:

- it has strong memetic weight but incomplete structural fit;
- it overlaps too heavily with another archetype;
- its historical associations distort the intended meaning;
- or multiple inherited figures remain plausible.

The repository should maintain a record of earlier names so conceptual development remains visible rather than being silently rewritten.

## Esoteric weight without obscurity

Esoteric knowledge is useful when it increases compression, not when it turns the model into a private code.

A successful name should operate at two levels:

1. A newcomer can grasp its basic human movement.
2. A deeper reader can discover additional mythic, ritual, dramatic, or metaphysical associations.

The symbol should be a door, not a password.

## The actor's test

A final practical test is:

> Can a skilled actor embody this figure and produce a recognizable human configuration without first reading a theoretical essay?

The actor should be able to infer:

- what the character serves;
- what they fear losing;
- what they notice first;
- how they obtain or protect what matters;
- and what happens to the body when the priority is threatened.

If the name cannot guide attention and embodiment, it may be a useful category but a weak archetype.
